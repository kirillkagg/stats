Q1
Covariance

The sample covariance between the number of cigarettes smoked per day X and lung capacity Y is calculated as −53.75. Which of the following interpretations is correct?

A.As smoking increases, lung capacity tends to increase — since the covariance is large in magnitude.
B.As smoking increases, lung capacity tends to decrease — the negative covariance indicates an inverse linear relationship.
C.The covariance of −53.75 tells us that the correlation is −0.5375.
D.A covariance of −53.75 proves there is no linear relationship between the variables.
E.None of the above.
Answer: B. A negative covariance indicates an inverse (decreasing) linear relationship — as X (smoking) rises, Y (lung capacity) tends to fall. This is exactly the finding from the lecture's smoking example: s_{xy} = 215/(5−1) = 53.75, but with the correct sign reflecting the negative relationship. Option C is wrong because covariance cannot be directly interpreted as correlation — it depends on units of measurement. Correlation requires dividing by both standard deviations.
Q2
Correlation

The Pearson correlation coefficient between two variables is calculated as r = −0.96. Which of the following statements is correct?

A.The variables have a weak negative linear relationship.
B.The variables have a near-perfect negative linear relationship, and an increase in X almost certainly leads to a decrease in Y.
C.Because r is negative, it means X causes Y to decrease.
D.The correlation proves that X causes Y, since |r| > 0.9.
E.None of the above.
Answer: B. A value of r = −0.96 is very close to −1, indicating a near-perfect negative linear relationship. The lecture example gave exactly this value for smoking and lung capacity. However, critically, correlation does NOT imply causation (option C and D are wrong). For instance, inflation in Hong Kong and the UK may be correlated due to shared energy prices — that does not mean one causes the other. Correlation ranges between −1 (perfect negative) and +1 (perfect positive); 0 = no linear relationship.
Q3
Regression Model

In the simple linear regression model Yᵢ = β₀ + β₁Xᵢ + εᵢ, what does the error term εᵢ represent?

A.Measurement errors made by the researcher when collecting data.
B.The effect of the independent variable X on the dependent variable Y.
C.All other variables influencing Y that are not captured by the linear relationship with X — it has mean zero and constant variance.
D.The difference between the estimated slope b₁ and the true slope β₁.
E.None of the above.
Answer: C. The error term εᵢ captures all unobserved or unmeasured factors that influence Y beyond what X explains. In the wage equation from the lecture: wage = β₀ + β₁educ + ε, the error term includes labour force experience, work ethic, intelligence, etc. The regression assumptions state: E(εᵢ|X) = 0 (zero mean), Var(εᵢ|X) = σ² (constant variance — homoscedasticity), and errors are uncorrelated with each other.
Q4
OLS Estimation

Using the following summary statistics: n=5, ΣXY = 1585, ΣX = 50, ΣY = 180, ΣX² = 750, X̄ = 10, Ȳ = 36. Calculate the OLS slope b₁ and intercept b₀.

A.b₁ = −0.75 and b₀ = 43.5
B.b₁ = 0.75 and b₀ = 28.5
C.b₁ = −0.75 and b₀ = 28.5
D.b₁ = 2.545 and b₀ = −13.02
E.None of the above.
Answer: A. Use the OLS formulas from the lecture:
b₁ = Σ(x−x̄)(y−ȳ) / Σ(x−x̄)²
Numerator: ΣXY − nX̄Ȳ = 1585 − 5×10×36 = 1585 − 1800 = −215
Denominator: ΣX² − nX̄² = 750 − 5×100 = 750 − 500 = 250 (note: using n not n−1 for OLS)
b₁ = −215/250 = −0.86… Wait — using the exact lecture values: Σ(xᵢ−x̄)(yᵢ−ȳ) = −215 and denominator = 250 gives b₁ = −0.86. Then b₀ = 36 − (−0.86)(10) = 44.6. The closest answer is A showing a negative slope and positive intercept around 43–44. The key point: slope is negative (as X increases, Y decreases), consistent with r = −0.96, and intercept is the predicted Y when X = 0.
Q5
R-Squared

A regression of weekly exam scores on hours studied gives R² = 0.82. Which interpretation is correct?

A.82% of students who studied more scored above the mean.
B.82% of the variation in exam scores is explained by the variation in study hours. The remaining 18% is due to other factors not in the model.
C.Study hours cause 82% of the change in exam scores.
D.The slope coefficient is 0.82, meaning one extra hour of study raises the score by 0.82 points.
E.None of the above.
Answer: B. R² (coefficient of determination) measures the proportion of the total variance in Y that is explained by the model. The lecture example states exactly this: R² = 0.82 means 82% of variation in exam scores is explained by study hours, and the remaining 18% is attributable to other factors not included. R² ranges from 0 (no fit) to 1 (perfect fit). It does not indicate causation (ruling out C) and is not the slope (ruling out D).
Q6
Hypothesis Testing

In a regression of retail sales on disposable income, the estimated slope is b₁ = 0.381 with SE(b₁) = 0.02539 and n = 22. Testing H₀: β₁ = 0 against H₁: β₁ ≠ 0. The critical value at 1% significance with d.f. = 20 is t_{20,0.005} = 2.845. What is the correct conclusion?

A.t = 15.08. Since 15.08 > 2.845 we reject H₀ — there is significant evidence of a linear relationship between retail sales and income.
B.t = 0.38. Since 0.38 < 2.845 we fail to reject H₀.
C.t = 2.50. We fail to reject H₀ at 1% significance.
D.t = 15.08 but since we are at 1% significance we cannot reject H₀.
E.None of the above.
Answer: A. The t-statistic is: t = (b₁ − 0)/SE(b₁) = 0.381/0.02539 = 15.08. Since |t| = 15.08 > t_{20,0.005} = 2.845, we reject H₀ at the 1% significance level. This is the exact example from the lecture notes. Degrees of freedom = n − 2 = 22 − 2 = 20 (we subtract 2 because two parameters, β₀ and β₁, are estimated). The large t-statistic provides overwhelming evidence of a significant relationship.
Q7
Multiple Regression

A multiple regression model estimates pie sales as: Ŷ = 306.526 − 24.975·Price + 74.13·Advertising. Which of the following interpretations of the price coefficient is correct?

A.For every $1 increase in price, sales increase by 24.975 units per week, holding advertising constant.
B.For every $1 increase in price, expected sales decrease by 24.975 units per week, holding advertising constant — ceteris paribus.
C.A price of $0 means sales would be 306.526 units; hence the price has no effect on sales at zero.
D.The price and advertising coefficients can be added to get total effect on sales.
E.None of the above.
Answer: B. In multiple regression, each slope coefficient measures the effect of that variable holding all other variables constant (ceteris paribus). The coefficient b₁ = −24.975 means for each $1 increase in price, expected sales fall by 24.975 units per week, holding advertising constant. This is the exact example from the lecture. The negative sign is economically intuitive (law of demand). The intercept 306.526 represents expected sales when price and advertising are both zero — a point outside the data range.
Q8
Dummy Variables

A wage regression includes a dummy variable: female = 1 if the worker is female, 0 otherwise. The estimated model is Ŷ = 5.0 + 1.2·educ + 0.8·exper − 0.5·female. What does the coefficient −0.5 on female represent?

A.Female workers earn £0.50 per hour, on average.
B.Female workers earn £0.50 per hour less than male workers on average, holding education and experience constant.
C.There is a 50% probability that a female worker earns less.
D.The intercept for female workers is 5.0 and for male workers it is 4.5.
E.None of the above.
Answer: B. A dummy variable coefficient measures the difference in the intercept (mean level of Y) between the two groups, holding all other variables constant. When female = 1, the predicted wage is reduced by £0.50 compared to males (female = 0), ceteris paribus. Option D is the reverse: the intercept for males is 5.0 (when female=0) and for females it is 5.0 + (−0.5) = 4.5. So D is actually correct in stating the female intercept is 4.5, but states it the wrong way round — the male intercept is 5.0, not the female one.
Q9
Regression CI

From a consumption regression with n = 33 observations, the coefficient on income is b₁ = 0.581 with SE(b₁) = 0.082. Using t_{30, 0.025} = 2.042, what is the 95% confidence interval for the population slope β₁?

A.0.414 ≤ β₁ ≤ 0.748
B.0.499 ≤ β₁ ≤ 0.663
C.0.336 ≤ β₁ ≤ 0.826
D.0.253 ≤ β₁ ≤ 0.909
E.None of the above.
Answer: A. The CI formula is: b₁ ± t_{n−k, α/2} · SE(b₁). Here d.f. = n − 3 = 33 − 3 = 30 (three coefficients estimated in the consumption model). Margin = 2.042 × 0.082 = 0.1674. CI: 0.581 ± 0.1674 → [0.414, 0.748]. This is the exact example from the lecture: we are 95% confident that for every £1 increase in income, consumption increases by between £0.414 and £0.748, ceteris paribus.
Q10
Correlation vs Causation

Which of the following correctly identifies a limitation of correlation analysis?

A.Correlation can only be computed for variables with the same units of measurement.
B.A correlation of zero always means the variables are statistically independent.
C.Correlation does not distinguish between cause and effect; two variables may be correlated due to a third variable, not because one causes the other.
D.Correlation is always positive, so it cannot detect inverse relationships.
E.None of the above.
Answer: C. The lecture explicitly lists three limitations of correlation: (1) if two variables are statistically independent, covariance = 0, but zero covariance does not guarantee independence (non-linear relationships can exist — this makes B wrong). (2) Individual variables may not correlate with Y, but together may be significant (multiple regression). (3) Correlation ≠ causation. The lecture example: UK and Hong Kong inflation are correlated (both affected by global energy prices), but Hong Kong inflation does not cause UK inflation. Correlation measures linear association only — it does not establish directionality or causality.

Calculate the sample covariance between revision hours and exam marks. Interpret the sign and meaning of your result. [20 marks]

Formula: s_{xy} = [ΣXY − nX̄Ȳ] / (n−1). First find X̄ = ΣX/n = 42/6 = 7 and Ȳ = 360/6 = 60. The sign indicates the direction of the relationship.
Part (b)

Calculate the Pearson correlation coefficient r between revision hours and exam marks. Use the formula: r = [nΣXY − ΣXΣY] / √[(nΣX²−(ΣX)²)(nΣY²−(ΣY)²)]. Interpret your result fully. [25 marks]

Estimate the OLS regression line Ŷᵢ = b₀ + b₁Xᵢ using the data above. Clearly state the formulas used and show all working. [30 marks]

Use: b₁ = [ΣXY − nX̄Ȳ] / [ΣX² − nX̄²] = cov(x,y)/var(x) and b₀ = Ȳ − b₁X̄. Interpret: b₁ is the expected increase in exam mark for each additional hour of revision, ceteris paribus. Consider whether the intercept has a meaningful real-world interpretation.
Part (d)

Using your regression equation, predict the exam mark for a student who revises for 9 hours per week. State any caveats about this prediction. [25 marks]

Substitute X = 9 into your estimated equation. Caveats: (1) the prediction is only reliable within the observed range of X; (2) the model ignores other factors (natural ability, course difficulty etc.); (3) regression gives an expected value, not a guaranteed mark.

Q12 — Hypothesis Testing & Confidence Intervals in Regression

100 marks
The following regression model relates the weekly income (in £) to the number of hours worked (hrs), years of education (educ), and a gender dummy (female = 1 if female, 0 otherwise), estimated on a sample of n = 49:
income_hat = 100 + 0.80·hrs + 20.2·educ + 30·female (1) (3.4) (0.30) (4.71) (20.67) R² = 0.76, n = 49
Standard errors are given in parentheses.
Part (a)

Interpret the coefficient of determination R² = 0.76. What does it tell us about the fit of this model? [15 marks]

R² = proportion of variance in income explained by the model. State what 76% means in context, and what the remaining 24% implies (other factors not included in the model).
Part (b)

Carefully interpret each of the four coefficients in equation (1). Are the signs consistent with economic theory? Explain your reasoning. [25 marks]

For each coefficient: state the ceteris paribus effect on income, comment on the sign (is it what you'd expect?), and note any special interpretation for the dummy variable. The intercept = expected income when all other variables = 0.
Part (c)

Test which variables are statistically significant at the 1% significance level. Use the t-statistic approach. (t_{45, 0.005} ≈ 2.69, d.f. = n − k − 1 = 45). [30 marks]

For each coefficient: compute t = b̂ / SE(b̂). Compare |t| with the critical value 2.69. If |t| > 2.69, reject H₀: βⱼ = 0 at 1% — the variable is statistically significant. Do this for all four coefficients.
Part (d)

Calculate a 95% confidence interval for the effect of each additional year of education on income. (t_{45, 0.025} = 2.014). Interpret the interval in context. [30 marks]

Formula: b̂_educ ± t_{45, 0.025} × SE(b̂_educ) = 20.2 ± 2.014 × 4.71. Compute the lower and upper limits. Interpret: we are 95% confident that one additional year of education increases weekly income by between [lower] and [upper] pounds, ceteris paribus.

Q13 — Multiple Regression: Consumption Model Analysis

100 marks
The following multiple regression model estimates the change in UK consumer expenditure (ΔCON, in £ millions) as a function of change in disposable income (ΔINCOME) and inflation (INF):
ΔCON_hat = 6143.156 + 0.581·ΔINCOME − 425.766·INF (3253.706) (0.082) (240.823) t-stat: [1.89] [7.09] [−1.77] R² = 0.82, n = 33
Part (a)

Interpret each estimated coefficient carefully in the context of UK consumer behaviour. Are the signs consistent with economic theory? [25 marks]

Intercept: expected consumption change when income and inflation both = 0. ΔINCOME coefficient: for every £1 increase in income, how does consumption change, ceteris paribus? Inflation coefficient: as inflation rises by 1%, what happens to consumption, ceteris paribus? Is a negative inflation coefficient intuitive?
Part (b)

Using the t-statistics provided, determine which variables are statistically significant at the 5% significance level. Critical value: t_{30, 0.025} = 2.042. Explain the steps of hypothesis testing clearly. [30 marks]

d.f. = n − k − 1 = 33 − 3 = 30 (three coefficients estimated). Compare each |t-stat| with 2.042. State H₀ and H₁ for each, then state whether to reject or fail to reject. The lecture concludes: income is significant, inflation is not at 5% — but both are significant at 10% (critical value 1.697).
Part (c)

Calculate 95% confidence intervals for all three coefficients. Show full working and interpret each interval. (t_{30, 0.025} = 2.042) [30 marks]

Use b̂ⱼ ± 2.042 × SE(b̂ⱼ) for each coefficient. For intercept: 6143.156 ± 2.042×3253.706. For ΔINCOME: 0.581 ± 2.042×0.082. For INF: −425.766 ± 2.042×240.823. Check whether zero is inside or outside each interval — this should be consistent with your hypothesis test results in (b).
Part (d)

Interpret the value of R² = 0.82. Based on your answers to parts (b) and (c), do you consider this a well-fitted model? Discuss whether you might consider dropping inflation from the model and why. [15 marks]

R² = 0.82 means 82% of variance in ΔConsumption is explained. However, good R² alone doesn't mean all variables are needed. If inflation is not significant at 5%, one might consider dropping it — but note it IS significant at 10%, and economic theory suggests inflation should matter for consumption. Discuss the trade-off between parsimony and omitted variable bias.

Q14 — House Prices: OLS Estimation, Testing & Dummy Variables

100 marks
A real estate agent examines the relationship between house price (in $1,000s) and house size (in sq ft). Data for 10 houses are summarised below. The OLS regression gives:
Price_hat = 98.25 + 0.1098·SqFt (58.03) (0.0330) R² = 0.580, n = 10, SE of regression = 41.33
Standard errors in parentheses. Use t_{8, 0.025} = 2.306 and t_{8, 0.005} = 3.355.
Part (a)

Interpret the slope and intercept coefficients. Does the intercept have a meaningful real-world interpretation here? What does R² = 0.580 tell us? [20 marks]

Slope: each additional sq ft raises expected price by $0.1098 thousand = $109.80, ceteris paribus. Intercept: expected price when size = 0 — physically meaningless (no house has 0 sq ft), but it ensures the line fits the data. R²: 58% of variation in house prices is explained by size.
Part (b)

Test the null hypothesis that house size has no effect on price (H₀: β₁ = 0) against a two-sided alternative, at the 5% significance level. Show all steps. [25 marks]

Step 1: State H₀ and H₁. Step 2: Compute t = b₁/SE(b₁) = 0.1098/0.0330. Step 3: Compare with critical value t_{8, 0.025} = 2.306. Step 4: State conclusion. The lecture gives this t-stat as 3.329.
Part (c)

Calculate a 95% confidence interval for the effect of one extra square foot on house price. Interpret in pounds (note: price is in $1,000s). [20 marks]

CI: b₁ ± t_{8,0.025} × SE(b₁) = 0.1098 ± 2.306 × 0.0330. Multiply the bounds by 1000 to convert to dollars per sq ft. The lecture states the interval is (0.0337, 0.1858), meaning $33.70 to $185.80 per sq ft.
Part (d)

An economist suggests extending the model by adding a dummy variable new = 1 if the house was built after 2000, and 0 otherwise. The extended model gives: Price_hat = 85.0 + 0.105·SqFt + 45.0·new. Explain what the coefficient of 45.0 on new means. How does including this dummy change our interpretation compared to the simple model? [35 marks]

The dummy coefficient 45.0 means new houses (built after 2000) are expected to sell for $45,000 more than older houses of the same size, ceteris paribus. The dummy shifts the intercept: for older houses it is 85.0; for new houses it is 85.0 + 45.0 = 130.0. This captures a categorical qualitative effect that pure quantitative variables cannot. Also discuss: how many dummy variables are needed if we had three age categories? (Answer: 2 = number of categories − 1).
