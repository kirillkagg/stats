Q1 Properties of the sampling distribution of the sample mean 5 marks
Which of the following statements about the sample mean X̄ is correct?
Topic: Sampling Distribution of X̄ — Properties (Slides 16–18)
  • A.X̄ is always normally distributed, regardless of sample size or population shape.
  • B.The variance of X̄ equals the population variance σ².
  • C.The variance of X̄ equals σ²/n, which decreases as sample size n increases.
  • D.The expected value of X̄ is equal to σ², the population variance.
  • E.None of the above.
✓ Correct Answer: C — Mark Scheme
2 marks — Correct option: C
3 marks — Explanation: The two fundamental properties of the sampling distribution of X̄ are:
E(X̄) = μ [X̄ is an unbiased estimator of the population mean] Var(X̄) = σ²/n [variance of X̄ equals population variance divided by n]
Option C is correct: Var(X̄) = σ²/n shrinks as n increases — this is the mathematical basis for why larger samples yield more precise estimates. The standard deviation of X̄ (the standard error) is σ_X̄ = σ/√n.
Why the others fail:
• A: X̄ is normally distributed only if the population is normal OR n is large enough (by the CLT). It is not always normal.
• B: Var(X̄) = σ²/n ≠ σ² (unless n = 1).
• D: E(X̄) = μ (the population mean), not σ².
Examiner's note: This is a high-frequency MCQ topic. Know both properties — E(X̄) and Var(X̄) — and be able to explain what they mean for statistical inference.
Q2 Standard error of the sample mean — calculation 5 marks
A population has mean μ = 120 and standard deviation σ = 18. A random sample of size n = 81 is drawn. What is the standard error of the sample mean?
Topic: Standard Error (Slides 18, 26–27)
  • A.18
  • B.9
  • C.2
  • D.0.22
  • E.None of the above.
✓ Correct Answer: C — Mark Scheme
2 marks — Correct option: C (Standard Error = 2)
3 marks — Explanation:
σ_X̄ = σ / √n = 18 / √81 = 18 / 9 = 2
The standard error measures the typical amount by which a sample mean deviates from the true population mean. With n = 81, √81 = 9, reducing σ = 18 by a factor of 9 to give SE = 2.
Option A (18) ignores the division by √n entirely — this is the population standard deviation, not the SE. Option B (9) is √81 alone (the denominator). Option D (0.22) mistakes the formula, dividing σ by n rather than √n.
Key insight: to halve the standard error you must quadruple the sample size, since SE ∝ 1/√n. Going from n = 81 to n = 324 would reduce SE from 2 to 1.
Examiner's note: Always apply σ_X̄ = σ/√n, not σ/n. The square root is the most commonly omitted step.
Q3 Sampling distribution — probability calculation (CLT) 5 marks
A large population has mean μ = 50 and standard deviation σ = 8. A random sample of n = 64 is taken. What is the probability that the sample mean is less than or equal to 51.5?
Topic: Z-transform of X̄ / CLT (Slides 26–36)
  • A.0.0668
  • B.0.4332
  • C.0.9332
  • D.0.5000
  • E.None of the above.
✓ Correct Answer: C — Mark Scheme
2 marks — Correct option: C (0.9332)
3 marks — Explanation: Since n = 64 ≥ 25, we apply the CLT: X̄ ~ N(μ, σ²/n).
Step 1 — Standard error: σ_X̄ = σ / √n = 8 / √64 = 8 / 8 = 1.0 Step 2 — Standardise: Z = (X̄ − μ) / σ_X̄ = (51.5 − 50) / 1.0 = 1.50 Step 3 — Look up Z-table: P(X̄ ≤ 51.5) = P(Z ≤ 1.50) = Φ(1.50) = 0.9332
Option A (0.0668) = P(Z > 1.50) — the upper tail, not the lower. Option B (0.4332) = Φ(1.50) − 0.5 — a table-reading error. Option D (0.5000) would only be correct if X̄ = μ = 50.
Examiner's note: Always show the three steps explicitly: (1) compute SE, (2) compute Z, (3) look up the table. Examiners award process marks even if you misread the table slightly.
Q4 Central Limit Theorem — conceptual statements 5 marks
Consider the following statements about the Central Limit Theorem (CLT). Which combination is correct?

(i) The CLT can be applied to both discrete and continuous random variables.
(ii) The CLT requires the population to be normally distributed before it can be applied.
(iii) The CLT states that the sampling distribution of X̄ approaches a normal distribution as n increases, regardless of the population's shape.
(iv) The CLT is fundamental to statistical inference because it allows us to make conclusions about population parameters using sample data alone.
Topic: Central Limit Theorem (Slides 31–38)
  • A.(i) and (ii) only.
  • B.(i), (iii), and (iv) only.
  • C.(ii) and (iii) only.
  • D.All of the above.
  • E.None of the above.
✓ Correct Answer: B — Mark Scheme
2 marks — Correct option: B — statements (i), (iii), and (iv)
3 marks — Explanation, statement by statement:
• (i) TRUE: The CLT applies whether the underlying population is discrete (e.g., Binomial, Poisson) or continuous (e.g., Uniform, Exponential). The type of the distribution does not restrict its applicability.
• (ii) FALSE: This is the key misconception. The CLT's power is precisely that it does not require the population to be normal — it states that normality of X̄ emerges from large n regardless of the population distribution. If the population is normal, X̄ is normal for any n, but this is a special case, not a requirement.
• (iii) TRUE: This is the formal statement of the CLT. For most non-normal distributions, n > 25 is sufficient for the approximation to be reliable.
• (iv) TRUE: The CLT is the cornerstone of classical inference (confidence intervals, hypothesis tests) precisely because it gives us the sampling distribution of X̄ without needing to know the exact population distribution.
Examiner's note: Statement (ii) is the deliberate trap in this question and appears in most exam years. The CLT is powerful because it bypasses the need for a normal population assumption.
Q5 Sampling distribution of the sample proportion 5 marks
The true proportion of adults who support a new environmental policy is P = 0.60. A random sample of n = 100 adults is drawn. What is the probability that the sample proportion p̂ exceeds 0.65?
Topic: Sampling Distribution of p̂ (Slides 39–42)
  • A.0.8461
  • B.0.3461
  • C.0.1539
  • D.0.6922
  • E.None of the above.
✓ Correct Answer: C — Mark Scheme
2 marks — Correct option: C (0.1539)
3 marks — Explanation:
Step 0 — Check normal approximation condition: n·P·(1−P) = 100 × 0.60 × 0.40 = 24 > 5 ✓ Step 1 — Standard error of p̂: σ_p̂ = √[P(1−P)/n] = √[0.60 × 0.40 / 100] = √0.0024 = 0.04899 Step 2 — Standardise: Z = (p̂ − P) / σ_p̂ = (0.65 − 0.60) / 0.04899 = 1.02 Step 3 — Table look-up: P(p̂ > 0.65) = P(Z > 1.02) = 1 − Φ(1.02) = 1 − 0.8461 = 0.1539
Option A (0.8461) = Φ(1.02) — the complement, i.e. P(p̂ ≤ 0.65). A classic sign error: "exceeds" means the upper tail. Option D (0.6922) ≈ P(−1.02 ≤ Z ≤ 1.02) — a two-tailed probability.
Examiner's note: Always check the condition nP(1−P) > 5 before applying the normal approximation to p̂. State this check explicitly — it earns marks and demonstrates understanding of when the approximation is valid.
Q6 Sampling distribution of sample variance — chi-squared 5 marks
A process produces items whose quality follows a normal distribution with population variance σ² = 2.25. A random sample of n = 20 batches is chosen. The probability that the sample variance S² exceeds 4.05 is:
Topic: Chi-squared distribution / Sample Variance (Slides 43–48)
  • A.Between 0.025 and 0.05.
  • B.Between 0.01 and 0.025.
  • C.Less than 0.01.
  • D.Greater than 0.05.
  • E.None of the above.
✓ Correct Answer: B — Mark Scheme
2 marks — Correct option: B — between 0.01 and 0.025
3 marks — Explanation: When sampling from a normal population, the statistic (n−1)S²/σ² follows a chi-squared distribution with n−1 degrees of freedom:
χ²_(n−1) = (n−1)S²/σ² Given: n = 20, σ² = 2.25, S² = 4.05, d.f. = 19 χ² = (20−1) × 4.05 / 2.25 = 19 × 1.80 = 34.20
From the chi-squared table with 19 degrees of freedom:
χ²_(19, 0.025) = 32.852 → P(χ² > 32.852) = 0.025 χ²_(19, 0.010) = 36.191 → P(χ² > 36.191) = 0.010 Since 32.852 < 34.20 < 36.191: P(S² > 4.05) = P(χ² > 34.20) is between 0.010 and 0.025
This indicates that a sample variance this extreme is unlikely (probability 1–2.5%) if the true population variance is 2.25, suggesting potential concern that the actual variability exceeds specifications.
Examiner's note: You must convert S² to the chi-squared statistic before using the table. The chi-squared distribution is right-skewed and does not have symmetric tails — never use the Z-table for variance inference.

Q7 Sampling Distribution of X̄, Standard Error & Central Limit Theorem 50 marks
This question tests your understanding of the sampling distribution of the sample mean, the standard error, the CLT, and probability calculations. Show all working and state all formulae.
(a) Standard Error & Sample Size25 marks
Why does sample size play such an important role in reducing the standard error of the mean? Using the formula σ_X̄ = σ/√n, explain what the implications are for statistical inference of increasing the sample size from n = 25 to n = 100 and then to n = 400, given a population with σ = 20.
✓ Model Answer — Part (a)
The standard error formula: σ_X̄ = σ/√n measures the typical deviation of the sample mean from the true population mean. It is the standard deviation of the sampling distribution of X̄.
n = 25: σ_X̄ = 20/√25 = 20/5 = 4.0 n = 100: σ_X̄ = 20/√100 = 20/10 = 2.0 n = 400: σ_X̄ = 20/√400 = 20/20 = 1.0
Why the square root? The variance of X̄ is Var(X̄) = σ²/n, which decreases linearly with n. Taking the square root introduces the √n denominator — a diminishing returns relationship. To halve the SE you must quadruple n, not double it. This has practical cost implications for survey design and data collection.
Implications for inference:
1. Precision: A smaller SE means the sample mean is a more reliable estimate of μ. With n = 400 vs n = 25, our estimate is four times more precise.
2. Confidence intervals: The width of a CI is proportional to SE. Larger n → narrower CI → more informative interval estimates.
3. Hypothesis testing: The test statistic Z = (X̄ − μ)/σ_X̄ becomes larger (in absolute value) for the same deviation (X̄ − μ) as n increases. Larger n increases the power of the test, making it easier to detect a true effect.
4. The normal approximation (CLT): Larger n also ensures the CLT applies, so the distribution of X̄ becomes approximately normal even if the population is not, enabling the use of Z-based methods.
5. Practical trade-off: While larger n always reduces SE, there are diminishing returns. The benefit of increasing n from 25 to 100 (SE falls by half) is larger than increasing from 100 to 400 (SE again halves). The marginal gain in precision must be weighed against the cost of collecting more data.
Mark allocation: Formula stated and applied numerically 6 marks + mathematical explanation of √n relationship 5 marks + each of five implications 2–3 marks = 25 marks. Credit any well-reasoned economic application.
(b) Normal Population — Probability Calculation25 marks
A large population of quarterly GDP growth rates follows a normal distribution with mean μ = 0.8% and standard deviation σ = 1.5%. A random sample of n = 25 quarters is drawn.
Part (i) [5 marks]
State the exact sampling distribution of X̄, giving the mean and variance. Why is no large-sample condition needed here?
Part (ii) [10 marks]
Find P(X̄ ≤ 1.1%). Show all working including the standardisation step.
Part (iii) [10 marks]
Find P(0.5% ≤ X̄ ≤ 1.4%). Sketch the relevant region under the standard normal curve and label all Z values.
✓ Worked Solution — Part (b)
(i) Sampling distribution: Since the population is normal, X̄ is exactly normally distributed for any n:
X̄ ~ N(μ, σ²/n) = N(0.8, 1.5²/25) = N(0.8, 0.09) σ_X̄ = √(σ²/n) = 1.5/√25 = 1.5/5 = 0.30
No large-sample CLT condition is required because linear combinations of independent normal random variables are also normally distributed. Normality of X̄ follows directly from the normality of the population — it holds exactly for any sample size, not just asymptotically.
(ii) P(X̄ ≤ 1.1%):
Z = (X̄ − μ) / σ_X̄ = (1.1 − 0.8) / 0.30 = 0.30 / 0.30 = 1.00 P(X̄ ≤ 1.1) = P(Z ≤ 1.00) = Φ(1.00) = 0.8413
(iii) P(0.5 ≤ X̄ ≤ 1.4):
Z₁ = (0.5 − 0.8) / 0.30 = −0.30/0.30 = −1.00 Z₂ = (1.4 − 0.8) / 0.30 = 0.60/0.30 = 2.00 P(0.5 ≤ X̄ ≤ 1.4) = P(−1.00 ≤ Z ≤ 2.00) = Φ(2.00) − Φ(−1.00) = 0.9772 − 0.1587 = 0.8185
Sketch: bell curve centred at 0, with the shaded region between Z = −1.00 and Z = 2.00. The region is not symmetric about 0 because the upper bound (Z = 2.00) is further from the centre than the lower bound (Z = −1.00).
Mark allocation: (i) Distribution stated 3 + reason 2 = 5. (ii) SE 2 + Z 4 + table correct 4 = 10. (iii) Both Z values 4 + table subtraction 4 + sketch/labels 2 = 10.
(c) CLT Application — Non-Normal Population25 marks
A large retail company records daily online sales. The distribution of daily sales is highly right-skewed (many small days, occasional large days) with mean μ = £12,000 and standard deviation σ = £4,800. A random sample of n = 64 trading days is selected.
Part (i) [5 marks]
Can the Central Limit Theorem be applied? Justify your answer and state the approximate sampling distribution of X̄.
Part (ii) [10 marks]
Find P(X̄ > £13,200). Interpret the result in plain language.
Part (iii) [10 marks]
Find the value c such that P(X̄ > c) = 0.025. What does this value represent in the context of the retailer's performance monitoring?
✓ Worked Solution — Part (c)
(i) Justification of CLT:
The population is highly right-skewed (not normal), so normality of X̄ is not automatic. However, n = 64 > 25, which is the standard threshold for the CLT to yield a reliable normal approximation for most distributions. Therefore, by the CLT:
σ_X̄ = σ/√n = 4800/√64 = 4800/8 = 600 X̄ ≈ N(12000, 600²) = N(12000, 360000)
(ii) P(X̄ > £13,200):
Z = (13200 − 12000) / 600 = 1200 / 600 = 2.00 P(X̄ > 13200) = P(Z > 2.00) = 1 − Φ(2.00) = 1 − 0.9772 = 0.0228
Interpretation: There is only a 2.28% probability that a random sample of 64 trading days would yield a mean daily sales figure above £13,200, if the true population mean is £12,000. Observing such a high average would be unusual and might prompt the retailer to investigate whether trading conditions have improved.
(iii) Find c such that P(X̄ > c) = 0.025:
P(X̄ > c) = 0.025 → P(Z > z) = 0.025 From Z-table: z = 1.96 [standard 97.5th percentile] Reverse transform: c = μ + z × σ_X̄ = 12000 + 1.96 × 600 = 12000 + 1176 = £13,176
Context: c = £13,176 is the 97.5th percentile of the sampling distribution of mean daily sales. If the retailer observes a sample mean above this value, there is only a 2.5% probability it arose by chance under the assumption μ = £12,000. This threshold could serve as an upper control limit in statistical process control — a signal that something unusual (positive or negative) may be driving sales.
Mark allocation: (i) n > 25 justification 2 + σ_X̄ 2 + distribution stated 1 = 5. (ii) Z 4 + table 4 + interpretation 2 = 10. (iii) Z = 1.96 stated 3 + reverse transform 5 + context 2 = 10.
Q8 Inferential Statistics, Sample Proportion & Sample Variance 50 marks
This question covers descriptive vs inferential statistics, the sampling distribution of the sample proportion, and the chi-squared distribution for sample variance inference. Show all working.
(a) Inferential Statistics & the CLT15 marks
Part (i) [7 marks]
Distinguish between descriptive statistics and inferential statistics. Provide one economic example of each. Why is statistical inference necessary?
Part (ii) [8 marks]
Why is the Central Limit Theorem described as one of the most important results in statistics? Discuss its conditions and explain why the requirement n > 25 is practically useful for economists.
✓ Model Answer — Part (a)
(i) Descriptive vs Inferential Statistics:
Descriptive statistics collect, summarise, and present data numerically and graphically — they describe what is observed in the data without drawing conclusions beyond it. Economic example: calculating the UK's mean GDP growth over the last decade; computing the Gini coefficient from a census.
Inferential statistics use sample data to draw conclusions (inferences) about a population. They involve estimation and hypothesis testing. Economic example: using a sample of 500 firms to test whether average productivity has increased since a policy change; using survey data to estimate the national unemployment rate with a confidence interval.
Inference is necessary because measuring the entire population is usually impossible (cost, time, destructive testing). Random samples allow reliable generalisation provided sampling is properly conducted.
(ii) Importance of the CLT:
The CLT states: given a population with mean μ and variance σ², the sampling distribution of X̄ based on a random sample of size n approaches N(μ, σ²/n) as n → ∞, regardless of the population's distribution. Formally:
Z = (X̄ − μ) / (σ/√n) → N(0,1) as n → ∞
It is "important" because: (1) it removes the need to know the exact population distribution — an enormous simplification; (2) it provides the theoretical foundation for Z-tests, t-tests, and confidence intervals; (3) it explains why normal-based methods work well in practice even when populations are not normal.
The n > 25 rule is practical for economists: real economic data (income, prices, returns) are often skewed, fat-tailed, or otherwise non-normal. The rule tells analysts that with modest sample sizes routinely achievable in surveys or datasets, the CLT approximation is valid enough for reliable inference — without requiring millions of observations.
Mark allocation: (i) Each definition 2 + example 1 + reason for inference 2 = 7. (ii) Formal statement 2 + three importance points 3 + n > 25 context 3 = 8.
(b) Sampling Distribution of Sample Proportion20 marks
An economist believes that 35% of consumers in a city prefer online shopping to in-store shopping. A random sample of n = 200 consumers is surveyed.
Part (i) [4 marks]
Verify that the normal approximation to p̂ is appropriate. State the sampling distribution of p̂, giving its mean and standard error.
Part (ii) [8 marks]
Find P(0.30 ≤ p̂ ≤ 0.40). Show all standardisation steps.
Part (iii) [8 marks]
Find the probability that the sample proportion exceeds 0.42. Interpret the result: if the survey returned p̂ = 0.42, should the economist revise the belief that P = 0.35?
✓ Worked Solution — Part (b)
(i) Check and distribution:
Condition: n·P·(1−P) = 200 × 0.35 × 0.65 = 45.5 > 5 ✓ E(p̂) = P = 0.35 σ_p̂ = √[P(1−P)/n] = √[0.35 × 0.65 / 200] = √[0.0011375] = 0.03373 p̂ ≈ N(0.35, 0.03373²)
(ii) P(0.30 ≤ p̂ ≤ 0.40):
Z₁ = (0.30 − 0.35) / 0.03373 = −0.05 / 0.03373 = −1.48 Z₂ = (0.40 − 0.35) / 0.03373 = 0.05 / 0.03373 = 1.48 P(0.30 ≤ p̂ ≤ 0.40) = P(−1.48 ≤ Z ≤ 1.48) = Φ(1.48) − Φ(−1.48) = 0.9306 − 0.0694 = 0.8612
There is approximately an 86.1% probability that the sample proportion falls within 5 percentage points of the true population proportion of 0.35.
(iii) P(p̂ > 0.42) and interpretation:
Z = (0.42 − 0.35) / 0.03373 = 0.07 / 0.03373 = 2.075 ≈ 2.08 P(p̂ > 0.42) = P(Z > 2.08) = 1 − Φ(2.08) = 1 − 0.9812 = 0.0188
Interpretation: If the true population proportion were P = 0.35, there would be only a 1.88% probability of obtaining a sample proportion as high as 0.42 in a sample of 200. This is below conventional significance levels (5% and even 2%). The economist has reasonable grounds to revise their belief — p̂ = 0.42 constitutes statistically meaningful evidence that the true proportion of online-preference consumers may be higher than 0.35. However, formal hypothesis testing would be needed to draw this conclusion rigorously.
Mark allocation: (i) Condition check 2 + distribution stated 2 = 4. (ii) Each Z value 2 + subtraction method 2 + final answer 2 = 8. (iii) Z + table 5 + interpretation 3 = 8.
(c) Sampling Distribution of Sample Variance — Chi-Squared15 marks
The number of hours per week spent on revision by economics students follows a normal distribution with a population standard deviation of σ = 4.5 hours. A random sample of n = 30 students is taken.
Part (i) [4 marks]
State the distribution of (n−1)S²/σ². What are the degrees of freedom? Why do we use n−1 rather than n?
Part (ii) [5 marks]
Is the probability more than 0.95 that the sample standard deviation S exceeds 3.5 hours? Show all calculations.
Part (iii) [6 marks]
Find the value K such that P(S² > K) = 0.05. Interpret this value: what would it tell a university if the observed S² from a sample of 30 students exceeded K?
✓ Worked Solution — Part (c)
(i) Distribution:
(n−1)S²/σ² ~ χ²_(n−1) Degrees of freedom = n − 1 = 30 − 1 = 29
We use n−1 (not n) because one degree of freedom is "used up" in estimating the population mean with X̄. The n deviations (Xᵢ − X̄) are subject to the constraint that they sum to zero — so only n−1 of them are free to vary independently. This makes S² an unbiased estimator of σ²: E(S²) = σ².
(ii) P(S > 3.5) — i.e., P(S² > 12.25):
χ² = (n−1)S²/σ² = 29 × 3.5² / 4.5² = 29 × 12.25 / 20.25 = 355.25 / 20.25 ≈ 17.54 From chi-squared table with d.f. = 29: χ²_(29, 0.95) = 17.71 → P(χ² < 17.71) = 0.95 → P(χ² > 17.71) = 0.05 Our χ² = 17.54 < 17.71, so P(χ² > 17.54) is slightly above 0.05. P(S > 3.5) = P(χ² > 17.54) ≈ 0.953 > 0.95 ✓
Yes — the probability is more than 0.95 that S exceeds 3.5 hours.
(iii) Find K where P(S² > K) = 0.05:
P(S² > K) = 0.05 → P(χ² > (n−1)K/σ²) = 0.05 From table: χ²_(29, 0.05) = 42.557 [upper 5% critical value] (n−1)K/σ² = 42.557 29 × K / 20.25 = 42.557 K = 42.557 × 20.25 / 29 = 861.78 / 29 ≈ 29.72 So K ≈ 29.72 (sample variance), giving s = √29.72 ≈ 5.45 hours
Interpretation: K = 29.72 is the upper 5% critical value of the sampling distribution of S² when σ² = 20.25. If the university observed S² > 29.72 from a sample of 30 students, there is strong statistical evidence (at the 5% significance level) that the true population variance exceeds σ² = 20.25 — i.e., that revision hours vary more widely than expected. This could prompt investigation into whether certain students are severely under-revising, and whether support interventions are needed.
Mark allocation: (i) Distribution stated 2 + degrees of freedom 1 + n−1 explanation 1 = 4. (ii) χ² calculation 3 + compare with critical value 2 = 5. (iii) Critical value identified 2 + K calculated 3 + interpretation 1 = 6.
