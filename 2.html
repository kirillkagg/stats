Q1 Random experiments and sample spaces 5 marks
Which of the following best describes a random experiment?
Topic: Experiments & Basic Outcomes (Slides 7–8)
  • A.A statistics lecturer always starts the lecture at exactly 9:00 am.
  • B.A firm increases its advertising budget by 15% and observes whether monthly sales rise or fall.
  • C.The sum of two plus two equals four.
  • D.A student revises for six hours and is guaranteed to pass the exam.
  • E.None of the above.
✓ Correct Answer: B — Mark Scheme
2 marks — Correct option: B
3 marks — Explanation: A random experiment has two defining properties: (i) it can be repeated (at least conceptually) under identical conditions, and (ii) its outcome cannot be predicted with certainty in advance. The set of all possible outcomes forms the sample space S.
Option B satisfies both: the firm can repeat this activity, and whether sales rise, fall, or stay flat is genuinely uncertain — S = {rise, fall, unchanged}. Options A, C, and D all have predetermined, certain outcomes by construction, so they are deterministic activities, not random experiments.
Examiner's note: Many students select "any activity with a result" — always link back to the definition. The key criterion is unpredictability of outcome, not complexity of the activity.
Q2 Counting methods — permutations vs combinations 5 marks
A university economics society has 9 members. It must elect a President, a Treasurer, and a Secretary (three distinct roles, one member per role). How many different ways can these three positions be filled?
Topic: Permutations (Slides 23–24)
  • A.84
  • B.504
  • C.729
  • D.27
  • E.None of the above.
✓ Correct Answer: B — Mark Scheme
2 marks — Correct option: B (504)
3 marks — Explanation: Because the three roles are distinct, the order of assignment matters — electing Alice as President and Bob as Treasurer is a different outcome from Bob as President and Alice as Treasurer. This is a permutation problem.
P(n, r) = n! / (n − r)! P(9, 3) = 9! / (9 − 3)! = 9 × 8 × 7 = 504
Option A (84) = C(9,3) — this is the answer for a committee with no distinct roles (order irrelevant). The distinction between permutations and combinations is critical here.
Examiner's note: Always identify first whether order matters. Named positions with distinct titles → permutations. Committee seats with no hierarchy → combinations.
Q3 Conditional probability 5 marks
Of the cars on a used car lot, 70% have air conditioning (AC) and 40% have a CD player (CD). 20% of all cars have both. What is the probability that a randomly selected car has a CD player, given that it has air conditioning?
Topic: Conditional Probability (Slides 36–38)
  • A.0.50
  • B.0.20
  • C.0.2857
  • D.0.40
  • E.None of the above.
✓ Correct Answer: C — Mark Scheme
2 marks — Correct option: C (0.2857)
3 marks — Explanation: Let A = car has AC (P(A) = 0.70), B = car has CD (P(B) = 0.40), P(A ∩ B) = 0.20. We want P(B | A):
P(B | A) = P(A ∩ B) / P(A) = 0.20 / 0.70 ≈ 0.2857
Intuition: conditioning on AC restricts the sample space to the 70% of cars with AC. Of all cars, 20% have both — so 20/70 ≈ 28.57% of the AC group also have a CD player.
Option A (0.50) is P(AC | CD) = 0.20/0.40 — the reversed conditioning event. Option D (0.40) is the unconditional P(CD). Never confuse P(A|B) with P(B|A).
Examiner's note: State the formula before substituting. Reversing the conditioning event — P(A|B) vs P(B|A) — is one of the most common errors in this topic.
Q4 Addition Rule — probability of a union 5 marks
A survey of 400 economics undergraduates finds: 220 study Microeconomics, 160 study Macroeconomics, and 80 study both. What is the probability that a randomly selected student studies at least one of the two subjects?
Topic: Addition Rule / Union of Events (Slides 33–34)
  • A.0.75
  • B.0.55
  • C.0.40
  • D.0.95
  • E.None of the above.
✓ Correct Answer: A — Mark Scheme
2 marks — Correct option: A (0.75)
3 marks — Explanation: "At least one" means one or the other or both — the union (∪). Using the General Addition Rule:
P(M) = 220/400 = 0.55 P(K) = 160/400 = 0.40 P(M ∩ K) = 80/400 = 0.20 P(M ∪ K) = P(M) + P(K) − P(M ∩ K) = 0.55 + 0.40 − 0.20 = 0.75
The subtraction of P(M ∩ K) corrects for double-counting the 80 students enrolled in both subjects. Option D (0.95) is the common error of forgetting this subtraction. Option B is simply P(Micro) alone.
Examiner's note: Write the formula first. The most common error is forgetting to subtract the intersection — which double-counts individuals belonging to both groups.
Q5 Statistical independence 5 marks
Events A and B have probabilities P(A) = 0.4, P(B) = 0.3, and P(A ∩ B) = 0.12. Which of the following statements is correct?
Topic: Statistical Independence (Slide 42)
  • A.Events A and B are mutually exclusive.
  • B.Events A and B are statistically independent.
  • C.Events A and B are collectively exhaustive.
  • D.P(A | B) = 0.12
  • E.None of the above.
✓ Correct Answer: B — Mark Scheme
2 marks — Correct option: B — Statistically independent
3 marks — Explanation: A and B are statistically independent if and only if P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B).
Check: P(A) × P(B) = 0.4 × 0.3 = 0.12 = P(A ∩ B) ✓ → Independent
Why the others fail:
• A (Mutually exclusive): Requires P(A ∩ B) = 0. Here 0.12 ≠ 0. ✗
• C (Collectively exhaustive): Requires P(A ∪ B) = 1. Here P(A ∪ B) = 0.4 + 0.3 − 0.12 = 0.58 ≠ 1. ✗
• D: P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B)/P(B) = 0.12/0.30 = 0.40 = P(A), which confirms independence — but P(A|B) = 0.40, not 0.12. ✗
Examiner's note: Never confuse independence with mutual exclusivity. If two events with positive probability are mutually exclusive, they cannot be independent — knowing one occurred rules out the other.
Q6 Bayes' Theorem — medical diagnostics 5 marks
A medical test detects a disease with 90% sensitivity (correctly identifies disease when present) and 85% specificity (correctly identifies healthy patients). The prevalence of the disease is 5%. A patient tests positive. What is the probability the patient actually has the disease?
Topic: Bayes' Theorem (Slides 49–53)
  • A.0.90
  • B.0.05
  • C.0.24
  • D.0.15
  • E.None of the above.
✓ Correct Answer: C — Mark Scheme
2 marks — Correct option: C (0.24)
3 marks — Explanation: Define D = has disease, D' = healthy, + = positive test.
Prior: P(D) = 0.05, P(D') = 0.95 Sensitivity: P(+|D) = 0.90 False pos: P(+|D') = 1 − 0.85 = 0.15 Law of Total Probability: P(+) = P(+|D)·P(D) + P(+|D')·P(D') = (0.90)(0.05) + (0.15)(0.95) = 0.045 + 0.1425 = 0.1875 Bayes' Theorem: P(D|+) = P(+|D) × P(D) / P(+) = 0.045 / 0.1875 = 0.24
Despite 90% test sensitivity, only 24% of positive patients actually have the disease — because prevalence is low (5%). This illustrates the importance of base rates: prior probabilities dramatically affect posterior beliefs. This is exactly why Bayes' Theorem matters in economics, finance, and medicine.
Examiner's note: Show the full Law of Total Probability step before applying Bayes. The counter-intuitive result (high test accuracy yet low posterior probability) is a classic application — discuss the role of the prior (prevalence) for full marks.

(c) Bayes' Theorem15 marks
A financial analyst assesses whether a company's stock will outperform the market. Historical data shows:
  • 35% of stocks outperform the market.
  • A proprietary indicator gives a "positive signal" in 70% of cases when a stock subsequently outperforms.
  • The indicator gives a positive signal in 25% of cases when stocks do not outperform.
Part (i) [4 marks]
Define all events. List all prior and conditional probabilities given in the question.
Part (ii) [5 marks]
Using the Law of Total Probability, calculate the probability that the indicator gives a positive signal.
Part (iii) [6 marks]
Given a positive signal, use Bayes' Theorem to find the revised probability of outperformance. Comment on how this compares to the prior and what this illustrates about Bayesian updating.
✓ Worked Solution — Part (c)
(i) Events and probabilities:
O = stock outperforms, P(O) = 0.35 O' = does not outperform, P(O') = 0.65 S = positive signal P(S | O) = 0.70 P(S | O') = 0.25
(ii) Law of Total Probability:
P(S) = P(S|O)·P(O) + P(S|O')·P(O') = (0.70)(0.35) + (0.25)(0.65) = 0.245 + 0.1625 = 0.4075
(iii) Bayes' Theorem:
P(O|S) = P(S|O) × P(O) / P(S) = 0.245 / 0.4075 ≈ 0.60
Interpretation: The prior probability was 0.35 (35%). After observing a positive signal, the posterior rises to 0.60 (60%) — a 25 percentage point upward revision. This demonstrates Bayesian updating: the indicator provides genuine information (it is not independent of outperformance), which shifts the analyst's beliefs. The stronger the signal's discriminatory power (gap between 0.70 and 0.25), the larger the revision. In financial contexts, such updates are central to portfolio and credit-risk analysis.
Mark allocation: (i) Events 2 + all probabilities listed 2 = 4. (ii) Formula 2 + P(S) 3 = 5. (iii) Correct posterior 3 + interpretation 3 = 6.
Q8 Probability Rules, Set Theory & Independence 50 marks
You must show all calculations and provide clear conceptual justification. Marks are awarded for correct reasoning as well as numerical answers.
(a) Conceptual Foundations15 marks
Part (i) [5 marks]
Explain, with an example, the difference between mutually exclusive events and statistically independent events. Can two events with positive probability be simultaneously mutually exclusive and independent? Justify your answer mathematically.
Part (ii) [5 marks]
Distinguish between collectively exhaustive events and complementary events. Provide one economic example of each.
Part (iii) [5 marks]
Compare the classical and relative frequency approaches to probability. In what circumstances would a macroeconomist prefer the relative frequency approach? Refer to the 2008 financial crisis in your answer.
✓ Model Answer — Part (a)
(i) Mutual exclusivity vs independence:
Mutually exclusive (ME): P(A ∩ B) = 0 — both events cannot occur simultaneously. Example: a firm either records a profit or a loss in a quarter (not both). Independent: P(A ∩ B) = P(A)·P(B) — occurrence of one does not affect the probability of the other. Example: whether a UK firm expands is independent of the outcome of an Australian rugby match.
Can they be both? NO. If ME: P(A ∩ B) = 0 If independent: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B) > 0 [since P(A), P(B) > 0] These are contradictory → two events with positive probability cannot be both mutually exclusive and statistically independent.
(ii) Collectively exhaustive vs complementary:
Collectively exhaustive: P(A₁ ∪ … ∪ Aₙ) = 1 — the set of events covers all possible outcomes. Example: GDP growth classified as {expansion, recession, stagnation} covers all states. Complementary: Ā is the complement of A — P(Ā) = 1 − P(A), and {A, Ā} are both ME and collectively exhaustive as a pair. Example: a trade deal is either ratified or it is not. Complementary events are a special case of collectively exhaustive events (with exactly two outcomes).
(iii) Classical vs relative frequency:
Classical: assigns probability based on equally likely outcomes and a finite sample space — suitable for symmetric lotteries, fair dice, card games. Relative frequency: assigns probabilities based on observed long-run frequencies from repeated experiments — P(A) = (frequency of A) / (total trials) as n → ∞.
A macroeconomist prefers relative frequency when outcomes are not equally likely or when no theoretical prior is available — e.g., estimating default rates on mortgage-backed securities, or the probability of a banking system collapse. The 2008 crisis arose partly because banks used classical (assumed normal) distributions for bundled mortgage risk, ignoring empirical evidence that house prices across regions were correlated — not independent as assumed. Relative frequency data would have revealed much higher joint default probabilities than theory predicted.
Mark allocation: Each sub-part: definition 2 + example 1 + analytical point 2 = 5. Total 15.
(b) Card Deck — Probability Rules20 marks
A standard deck of 52 playing cards is shuffled. One card is drawn at random. Let J = "card is a Jack" and H = "card is a Heart".
Part (i) [4 marks]
Find P(J), P(H), and P(J ∩ H). Briefly explain what P(J ∩ H) represents.
Part (ii) [5 marks]
Find P(J ∪ H) using the General Addition Rule. Interpret this probability in plain language.
Part (iii) [5 marks]
Are J and H mutually exclusive? Are they statistically independent? Provide a mathematical test for each and state your conclusion.
Part (iv) [6 marks]
Find P(J | H) and P(H | J). Why do these two answers differ? What does this illustrate about the symmetry (or lack thereof) of conditional probability?
✓ Worked Solution — Part (b)
(i)
P(J) = 4/52 = 1/13 ≈ 0.0769 [4 Jacks in 52 cards] P(H) = 13/52 = 1/4 = 0.25 [13 Hearts in 52 cards] P(J ∩ H) = 1/52 ≈ 0.0192 [only the Jack of Hearts]
P(J ∩ H) is the probability of drawing the single card that is simultaneously a Jack AND a Heart — the Jack of Hearts.
(ii) General Addition Rule:
P(J ∪ H) = P(J) + P(H) − P(J ∩ H) = 4/52 + 13/52 − 1/52 = 16/52 = 4/13 ≈ 0.3077
Interpretation: There is approximately a 30.8% probability the drawn card is either a Jack, a Heart, or the Jack of Hearts (i.e., at least one of the two properties).
(iii)
Mutually exclusive? Condition: P(J ∩ H) = 0 Check: P(J ∩ H) = 1/52 ≠ 0 → NOT mutually exclusive ✗ (The Jack of Hearts belongs to both events simultaneously.) Independent? Condition: P(J ∩ H) = P(J) × P(H) Check: P(J) × P(H) = (1/13)(1/4) = 1/52 = P(J ∩ H) ✓ → J and H ARE statistically independent ✓
Intuition: knowing the card is a Heart tells you nothing about whether it is a Jack — there is one Jack among Hearts, just as there is one Jack in any other suit. The suit and the rank are unrelated properties of a playing card.
(iv)
P(J | H) = P(J ∩ H) / P(H) = (1/52) / (13/52) = 1/13 ≈ 0.077 P(H | J) = P(J ∩ H) / P(J) = (1/52) / (4/52) = 1/4 = 0.25
These differ because the conditioning events have different sizes: there are 13 Hearts but only 4 Jacks. Conditioning on Hearts (a large subset of 13) makes a Jack rare within that subset (1 in 13). Conditioning on Jacks (a small subset of 4) gives each Jack a 1-in-4 chance of being a Heart. Conditional probability is not symmetric: P(A|B) ≠ P(B|A) in general, and the reversal requires Bayes' Theorem.

(c) Bayes' Theorem — Oil Drilling15 marks
A drilling company estimates a 40% prior probability of striking oil at a new well. A geological survey (S) can be commissioned. Historical records show:
  • 60% of successful wells had a geological survey scheduled.
  • 20% of unsuccessful wells had a geological survey scheduled.
Part (i) [5 marks]
The well has been scheduled for a geological survey. Using Bayes' Theorem, calculate the revised (posterior) probability that the well will be successful.
Part (ii) [5 marks]
Construct a probability tree diagram showing all prior probabilities, conditional probabilities, joint probabilities, and the marginal probability P(S).
Part (iii) [5 marks]
The company's decision rule is: "Proceed with drilling if P(success | survey scheduled) > 0.50." Based on your answer to (i), should the company proceed? Comment on two limitations of using this rule in isolation.
✓ Worked Solution — Part (c)
(i) Setup and Bayes' Theorem:
W = well successful, P(W) = 0.40 W' = well fails, P(W') = 0.60 S = survey scheduled P(S | W) = 0.60 P(S | W') = 0.20 Law of Total Probability: P(S) = P(S|W)·P(W) + P(S|W')·P(W') = (0.60)(0.40) + (0.20)(0.60) = 0.24 + 0.12 = 0.36 Bayes' Theorem: P(W | S) = [P(S|W) × P(W)] / P(S) = 0.24 / 0.36 = 0.667 ≈ 0.67
The survey revises the probability of success from 0.40 (prior) to 0.67 (posterior).
(ii) Probability tree:
┌─ Survey (S) P=0.60 → Joint: 0.40 × 0.60 = 0.240 Start ─ W (0.40) ──┤ └─ No survey P=0.40 → Joint: 0.40 × 0.40 = 0.160 ┌─ Survey (S) P=0.20 → Joint: 0.60 × 0.20 = 0.120 ─ W' (0.60) ─┤ └─ No survey P=0.80 → Joint: 0.60 × 0.80 = 0.480 ───── 1.000 P(S) = 0.240 + 0.120 = 0.360
(iii) Since P(W | S) = 0.667 > 0.50, the decision rule recommends proceeding with drilling.
Limitations:
1. Ignores expected payoffs and costs: Even with 66.7% probability of success, if the cost of a dry well is very high relative to the profit from oil, the expected monetary value (EMV) may be negative. A probability threshold alone cannot substitute for a full expected value analysis.
2. Historical priors may not apply: The 40% prior and the conditional probabilities for the survey are based on historical data. If geological conditions at this specific site differ materially from historical wells, or if the survey method has changed, the priors are unreliable. Sensitivity analysis on the priors is essential before committing to a drilling decision.
Mark allocation: (i) Setup 2 + P(S) 2 + posterior 1 = 5. (ii) Correct tree structure 3 + all joint probabilities 2 = 5. (iii) Decision 1 + each limitation 2 = 5.
