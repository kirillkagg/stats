Q1Population · Sample · Parameter · Statistic
Which of the following statements correctly distinguishes between a population parameter and a sample statistic?
  • A parameter is computed from sample data; a statistic describes the entire population.
  • A parameter is a numerical value describing a characteristic of a population; a statistic is a numerical value describing a characteristic of a sample.
  • A statistic is always more accurate than a parameter because it is derived from observed data.
  • Both parameters and statistics are used exclusively to describe populations.
  • None of the above.
✓ Correct answer: B
A parameter (e.g. population mean μ, population variance σ²) is a fixed but often unknown value describing the entire population. A statistic (e.g. sample mean x̄, sample variance s²) is calculated from observed sample data and used to estimate the unknown parameter. Option A reverses these definitions. Option C is false — a statistic is subject to sampling variability and is not necessarily more accurate. Option D is incorrect; statistics describe samples, not populations.
Q2Descriptive vs Inferential Statistics
A bank calculates the average monthly balance of all 5,000 of its own current account holders and publishes this in its annual report. A researcher then uses a random sample of 200 of these accounts to estimate the average monthly balance of all UK current account holders. Which statement correctly classifies these two actions?
  • Both are examples of inferential statistics, since both involve numerical computation.
  • The first action is descriptive statistics; the second is inferential statistics.
  • The first action is inferential statistics, because it uses all 5,000 accounts to draw a conclusion.
  • The second action is descriptive statistics, since it summarises the 200 accounts in the sample.
  • None of the above.
✓ Correct answer: B
Descriptive statistics summarise the data observed — computing the mean balance of the bank's own 5,000 customers describes only those customers. Inferential statistics use sample data to draw conclusions about a broader population — the researcher's goal of estimating the UK-wide average from 200 accounts goes beyond the observed data. Option C is wrong: using an entire sub-population descriptively does not make it inferential. Option D is wrong because the researcher's intent is inference, not mere summary.
Q3Data Classification
Consider the following four variables: (i) the number of employees in a firm, (ii) the annual revenue of a firm in £, (iii) a firm's credit rating (AAA, AA, A, BBB…), and (iv) the sector a firm belongs to (e.g. Technology, Finance, Healthcare). Which of the following correctly classifies these variables?
  • (i) and (ii) are continuous; (iii) and (iv) are qualitative.
  • (i) is discrete quantitative; (ii) is continuous quantitative; (iii) is ordinal qualitative; (iv) is nominal qualitative.
  • (i), (ii), and (iii) are quantitative; only (iv) is qualitative.
  • (i) and (ii) are discrete; (iii) and (iv) are continuous.
  • None of the above.
✓ Correct answer: B
(i) Number of employees — discrete quantitative: it takes only whole number values (you cannot have 12.5 employees). (ii) Annual revenue — continuous quantitative: it can take any positive real value (£1,234,567.89…). (iii) Credit rating — ordinal qualitative: the categories have a meaningful order (AAA > AA > A…) but the gap between ratings is not numerically defined. (iv) Sector — nominal qualitative: categories with no inherent ordering. Option A is wrong because (i) is discrete, not continuous. Option C incorrectly treats credit ratings as quantitative.
Q4Central Tendency · Skewness
The annual bonuses (£000s) paid to eight traders at an investment bank are: 10, 12, 14, 15, 16, 18, 20, 95. Which of the following statements is correct?
  • The distribution is negatively skewed, since the mean is pulled below the median by the outlier.
  • The mean, median, and mode are all equal, indicating a symmetric distribution.
  • The distribution is positively skewed; the mean exceeds the median because the extreme value of £95,000 pulls the mean upward to the right.
  • The median is the least appropriate measure of central tendency here because it ignores the extreme value.
  • None of the above.
✓ Correct answer: C
Mean = (10+12+14+15+16+18+20+95)/8 = 200/8 = £25,000
Median = average of 4th and 5th values (ranked) = (15+16)/2 = £15,500

The mean (£25,000) > median (£15,500) — the classic signature of a positively skewed (right-skewed) distribution, where the tail extends to the right. The outlier bonus of £95,000 pulls the mean far above most of the data. In a positively skewed distribution: Mode < Median < Mean. Option A is wrong (it is positively, not negatively, skewed). Option D is incorrect — the median is actually preferred here precisely because it is robust to outliers.
Q5Sample Variance · Why n−1
A sample of four observations has values: 3, 7, 7, 19. Consider the following two statements:

(i) The sample variance is 48, calculated by dividing by (n−1) = 3.
(ii) Dividing by n−1 rather than n produces an unbiased estimator of the population variance.
  • Statement (i) is correct; statement (ii) is false — dividing by n−1 introduces a downward bias.
  • Statement (i) is correct; statement (ii) is also correct.
  • Statement (i) is false (the correct sample variance is 36); statement (ii) is correct.
  • Both statements are false.
  • None of the above.
✓ Correct answer: B
x̄ = (3+7+7+19)/4 = 36/4 = 9
Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² = (3−9)² + (7−9)² + (7−9)² + (19−9)² = 36+4+4+100 = 144
s² = 144/(4−1) = 144/3 = 48 → Statement (i) ✓

Statement (ii) is also correct. When computing a sample variance, the deviations are calculated from the sample mean x̄ rather than the true (unknown) population mean μ. Because x̄ is the value that minimises the sum of squared deviations, using it tends to underestimate the true spread. Dividing by n−1 instead of n corrects for this, producing an unbiased estimator of σ². This is sometimes called Bessel's correction. Note: 36 (option C) would be the result of dividing by n = 4, which gives the biased estimate.
Q6Coefficient of Variation★ High exam probability
Over the past five years, Stock X has averaged an annual return of 20% with a standard deviation of 6%. Stock Y has averaged an annual return of 15% with a standard deviation of 6%. Which of the following is correct?
  • Stock X has smaller relative variation than Stock Y.
  • Stock Y has smaller relative variation than Stock X.
  • Both stocks exhibit identical relative variation because they have the same standard deviation.
  • The coefficient of variation cannot be used to compare stocks with different means.
  • None of the above.

CV = (S / X̄) × 100

✓ Correct answer: A
CVX = (6/20) × 100 = 30%
CVY = (6/15) × 100 = 40%

Despite identical standard deviations, Stock X carries less risk per unit of return (CV = 30% vs 40%). This illustrates why the CV — not the raw standard deviation — is the correct measure when comparing variability across data sets with different means. Option C is a very common error: equal standard deviations do not imply equal relative variation when the means differ. Option D is wrong — the whole point of the CV is precisely to allow comparisons across different-scale data.
Q7Interquartile Range
The following seven values represent the daily trading volumes (in millions of shares) at a stock exchange, ranked in ascending order: 3, 7, 9, 12, 15, 18, 24. Consider the following statements:

(i) The interquartile range (IQR) is 11.
(ii) The IQR eliminates the effect of outliers by measuring the spread of the middle 50% of the data.
(iii) Q1 = 7 and Q3 = 18.
  • Statements (i) and (iii) only are correct.
  • All three statements are correct.
  • Statements (i) and (ii) only are correct.
  • Statement (ii) only is correct.
  • None of the above.
✓ Correct answer: B
With n = 7 ranked observations: 3, 7, 9, 12, 15, 18, 24
Median (Q2) = 4th value = 12
Lower half (below median): 3, 7, 9 → Q1 = median = 7
Upper half (above median): 15, 18, 24 → Q3 = median = 18
IQR = Q3 − Q1 = 18 − 7 = 11

All three statements are correct. The IQR specifically measures the range within which the middle 50% of observations fall, making it robust to extreme values at either end of the distribution — unlike the full range, which is entirely determined by the two most extreme values.
Q8Correlation Coefficient
An analyst computes the following for two assets X and Y: Cov(X, Y) = −15, σX = 5, σY = 6. Which of the following correctly describes the relationship between X and Y?
  • The correlation coefficient is −0.50, indicating a perfect negative linear relationship.
  • The correlation coefficient is −0.50, indicating a moderate negative linear relationship.
  • The correlation coefficient is 0.50, since correlation cannot be negative.
  • The covariance of −15 is sufficient to conclude there is a strong negative relationship; the correlation is unnecessary.
  • None of the above.

r = Cov(X,Y) / (σX · σY)

✓ Correct answer: B
r = Cov(X,Y) / (σX · σY) = −15 / (5 × 6) = −15/30 = −0.50

A correlation of −0.50 indicates a moderate negative linear relationship — when X increases, Y tends to decrease, but not perfectly. A perfect negative relationship would be r = −1.00. Option A is numerically correct but wrong in its interpretation. Option C is false — correlation can range from −1 to +1. Option D illustrates why covariance alone is insufficient: covariance is scale-dependent (its value changes if the units of X or Y change), whereas the correlation coefficient is standardised and always lies between −1 and +1, making it the appropriate measure for comparing strength of relationships.

Q9
An investment analyst is comparing two equity portfolios. The annual returns (%) for each portfolio over the last five years are given below:
Year12345
Portfolio A4681012
Portfolio B398713
(a) 20 marks
Explain the difference between descriptive and inferential statistics. Using the investment context above, give one example of each type of analysis. Why is it important to distinguish between a population parameter and a sample statistic when drawing conclusions from financial data?
Model answer — key points
Descriptive statistics summarise and present what was observed in a data set, using measures such as the mean, variance, and graphical tools (histograms, bar charts). They make no claims beyond the data collected.

Inferential statistics use sample data to draw conclusions or make estimates about a wider, unobserved population. They involve estimation and hypothesis testing.

Example (descriptive): Computing the mean annual return of Portfolio A over these five years (= 8%) — this describes only the five observed years.
Example (inferential): Using these five years to estimate the portfolio's long-run expected return, or to test whether Portfolio A and Portfolio B have the same true mean return.

The distinction between parameter (e.g. the true long-run mean return μ, unknown) and statistic (e.g. the sample mean x̄ = 8%, computed from only five years) matters because statistics are subject to sampling variability. A different five-year window would produce a different x̄. Treating sample statistics as if they were true population parameters — without accounting for uncertainty — can lead to overconfident investment decisions.

(b)(ii) 20 marks
Calculate the coefficient of variation for each portfolio. Which portfolio carries greater relative risk per unit of return? Explain why the CV is a more appropriate measure of risk here than comparing standard deviations alone.

CV = (s / x̄) × 100

Model answer
sA = √10 ≈ 3.16  |  sB = √13 ≈ 3.61
CVA = (3.16/8.00) × 100 = 39.53%
CVB = (3.61/8.00) × 100 = 45.07%

Portfolio B carries greater relative risk (CV = 45.07% vs 39.53%). For every 1% of expected return, Portfolio B is more volatile.

The CV is more appropriate than raw standard deviations here because it expresses variability as a proportion of the mean, allowing fair comparison. If two portfolios had different mean returns (e.g. one averaged 8% and the other 20%), a direct comparison of standard deviations would be misleading — a larger standard deviation in the higher-return portfolio might still represent proportionally less risk.

(b)(iv) 20 marks
Using your answers to parts (b)(i) and (b)(iii), calculate the correlation coefficient between Portfolio A and Portfolio B. Interpret your result and explain what advantage the correlation coefficient has over the covariance when assessing the relationship between two variables.

r = Cov(A,B) / (sA · sB)

Model answer
From previous parts: Cov(A,B) = 9, sA = 3.16, sB = 3.61
r = 9 / (3.16 × 3.61) = 9 / 11.41 = 0.79

A correlation of +0.79 indicates a strong positive linear relationship between the two portfolios — they move together closely but not perfectly. This confirms the interpretation from the covariance but adds precision about the strength of the relationship.

Advantage of correlation over covariance: The covariance (9.00) is scale-dependent — its value would change if returns were expressed in different units, making comparison across different pairs of assets meaningless. The correlation coefficient is standardised to always lie between −1 and +1, regardless of the units or scale of the original variables. This makes it directly interpretable: r = +1 is a perfect positive relationship, r = 0 is no linear relationship, and r = −1 is a perfect negative relationship.
