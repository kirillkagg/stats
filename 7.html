Q11 Dependent Samples — Weight Loss Programme [25 marks]

A nutritionist evaluates a 12-week dietary intervention by recording the body weight (in kg) of 7 participants before and after the programme:

ParticipantBefore (xi)After (yi)
19285
2110104
37875
49590
58380
610199
78879
(a) [15 marks]

Compute the sample mean difference d̄ and the sample standard deviation of differences sd. Define clearly what the paired difference di represents in this context.

Differences di = xi − yi (weight before minus weight after):
d: 7, 6, 3, 5, 3, 2, 9 → Sum = 35

d̄ = 35/7 = 5.00 kg

Deviations from mean: 2, 1, −2, 0, −2, −3, 4
Squared deviations: 4, 1, 4, 0, 4, 9, 16 → Sum = 38

sd = √(38/6) = √6.333 ≈ 2.52

Interpretation: On average, participants lost 5 kg over the programme. The paired difference removes between-subject variation, isolating the programme's effect on each individual.
(b) [25 marks]

Construct a 95% confidence interval for the population mean paired difference μd. Use t6, 0.025 = 2.447. State the formula used and interpret your interval.

Formula: d̄ ± tn−1, α/2 · (sd/√n)

Margin of error: 2.447 × (2.52/√7) = 2.447 × 0.9526 = 2.33

95% CI: 5.00 ± 2.33 → (2.67, 7.33)

Interpretation: We are 95% confident that the true mean weight loss from the programme lies between 2.67 kg and 7.33 kg. Since the interval does not contain zero, the programme produces a statistically significant weight reduction at the 5% level.
(c) [10 marks]

Why is a matched-pairs (dependent samples) design preferred over using two independent groups in this study? What conditions must hold for the procedure in (b) to be valid?

Why matched pairs? Pairing removes between-subject variation (e.g. differences in initial metabolism, lifestyle). Each person acts as their own control, so any change measured reflects the programme's effect rather than pre-existing differences between individuals. This increases statistical power.

Conditions for validity: (1) The population of differences di must be normally distributed — plausible here with n=7. (2) The differences must be an independent random sample. (3) Each pair must consist of the same individual (or truly matched units) before and after.
Q12 Independent Samples — Known Population Variances [25 marks]

A manufacturer compares the daily output of two production lines, A and B. From historical records, the population standard deviation for line A is 8.4 units/hour and for line B is 11.3 units/hour. Independent random samples give the following results:

Line ALine B
Sample size4036
Sample mean (units/hour)130120
(a) [20 marks]

Construct a 95% confidence interval for the difference in mean hourly production between Line A and Line B (μA − μB). Clearly state the formula and interpret your findings.

Since population variances are known, use Z:
(x̄ − ȳ) ± zα/2 · √(σA2/nA + σB2/nB)

SE: √(8.4²/40 + 11.3²/36) = √(1.764 + 3.547) = √5.311 = 2.305

Margin: 1.96 × 2.305 = 4.52

95% CI: (130−120) ± 4.52 = 10 ± 4.52 → (5.48, 14.52)

Interpretation: We are 95% confident that Line A produces between 5.48 and 14.52 more units per hour than Line B on average. Since the entire interval is positive and excludes zero, there is a statistically significant difference in productivity favouring Line A.
(b) [15 marks]

State the two key assumptions required for the confidence interval in (a) to be valid. Would the procedure change if the sample sizes were small (say, nA = 8, nB = 7)? Explain.

Assumptions: (1) Samples are drawn randomly and independently from the two populations. (2) Both population distributions are normal (or sample sizes are large enough to invoke the CLT — satisfied here with n=40 and n=36).

Small sample change: With nA=8 and nB=7, the CLT no longer applies. We would still use the Z-statistic only if both populations are normally distributed AND the population variances are truly known. In practice with small samples, we rarely know σ with certainty, so we would instead use the t-statistic with pooled or separate variance, depending on the equality assumption.
Q13 Independent Samples — Unknown Equal Variances [25 marks]

A publishing company investigates first-year sales of textbooks with and without supplementary data files. From 20 textbooks, 10 are selected at random to contain supplementary files and 10 do not. Results are:

With files (X)Without files (Y)
Sample size1010
Mean sales (units)9,2548,167
Sample std dev2,1071,681

Assume first-year sales in both groups are normally distributed with equal (unknown) population variances.

(a) [20 marks]

Calculate the pooled variance sp2 and construct a 99% confidence interval for μX − μY. Use t18, 0.005 = 2.878. State the number of degrees of freedom clearly.

Step 1 — Pooled variance (df = nx + ny − 2 = 18):
sp2 = [(10−1)(2107²) + (10−1)(1681²)] / 18
= [9 × 4,439,449 + 9 × 2,825,761] / 18
= [39,955,041 + 25,431,849] / 18 = 65,386,890 / 18 = 3,632,605

Step 2 — Standard error:
√(sp2/10 + sp2/10) = √(363,260.5 + 363,260.5) = √726,521 ≈ 852.36

Step 3 — CI:
(9254 − 8167) ± 2.878 × 852.36 = 1087 ± 2453.09
→ (−1366.09, 3540.09)

Interpretation: The 99% CI contains zero, so we cannot conclude there is a statistically significant difference in mean sales at the 1% level.
(b) [15 marks]

Why do we use a pooled variance when the two population variances are assumed equal? Explain what information is "pooled" and why weighting by (n − 1) is appropriate rather than simply averaging sx2 and sy2.

If both populations share the same true variance σ², then both sx2 and sy2 are estimates of σ². Using only one would waste the information in the other sample.

Simple averaging (i.e. (sx2 + sy2)/2) is only appropriate when sample sizes are equal — it gives each sample equal weight regardless of how much data it contains. When nx ≠ ny, the larger sample provides more information about σ², so it should contribute proportionally more. Weighting by (n − 1) — the degrees of freedom for each variance estimate — achieves this in an unbiased way, since the estimator s2 is based on (n − 1) independent squared deviations.
Q14 Two Population Proportions (Large Samples) [25 marks]

A political campaign manager wants to assess the difference in candidate approval between two cities. In City X, 310 of 600 residents surveyed expressed approval. In City Y, 195 of 400 residents expressed approval.

(a) [10 marks]

Calculate the sample proportions p̂x and p̂y and compute the standard error of the difference p̂x − p̂y.

p̂x = 310/600 = 0.5167
p̂y = 195/400 = 0.4875

Standard error:
SE = √[p̂x(1−p̂x)/nx + p̂y(1−p̂y)/ny]
= √[(0.5167 × 0.4833)/600 + (0.4875 × 0.5125)/400]
= √[0.000416 + 0.000625]
= √0.001041 ≈ 0.0323
(b) [25 marks]

Construct a 98% confidence interval for the difference in population proportions (px − py). Use z0.01 = 2.326. Interpret your result fully and state what conclusion the campaign manager can draw.

Formula: (p̂x − p̂y) ± zα/2 · SE

Point estimate: 0.5167 − 0.4875 = 0.0292
Margin of error: 2.326 × 0.0323 = 0.0751

98% CI: 0.0292 ± 0.0751 → (−0.0459, 0.1043)

Interpretation: We are 98% confident that the true difference in approval proportions between City X and City Y lies between −0.05 and 0.10. Since the interval contains zero, the data do not provide sufficient evidence at the 2% significance level of a statistically significant difference in candidate approval between the two cities. The campaign manager cannot claim that approval differs meaningfully across the two cities based on this sample.
(c) [15 marks]

What is the minimum combined sample size condition that should be satisfied before applying the large-sample CI for the difference between two proportions? Is this condition met here? What happens if we apply the formula when sample sizes are small?

Rule of thumb: Each sample should contain at least 40 observations (some textbooks say each of nx·p̂x, nx(1−p̂x), ny·p̂y, ny(1−p̂y) ≥ 5). Here nx = 600 and ny = 400, so the condition is clearly satisfied.

Small sample failure: The derivation relies on the Central Limit Theorem so that (p̂x − p̂y) is approximately normally distributed. With small n, this approximation breaks down — the actual sampling distribution may be skewed, the Z critical value is invalid, and the resulting interval will have incorrect coverage probability (it may be too narrow or too wide). In that case, exact methods (e.g. Fisher's exact test) should be used instead.
